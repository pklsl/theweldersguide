# Resistance Spot Welding: How It Works and When to Use It

If you have ever assembled sheet metal, worked on automotive body repair, or walked past a robot welding line in an automotive factory, you have seen resistance spot welding in action. It is the most widely used welding process in manufacturing — the auto industry alone uses billions of spot welds per year. Yet it is not widely understood outside manufacturing circles.

This guide covers how spot welding works, its applications, and what you need to know to use it in a home shop or auto body setting.

## The Principle of Resistance Spot Welding

Resistance spot welding (RSW) works on a simple principle: **electrical resistance generates heat**. When a large current passes through a resistive path, heat is generated in proportion to the resistance. In spot welding, that resistance path is the interface between two metal sheets.

### The Spot Welding Cycle

1. **Position:** Two copper electrodes clamp the metal sheets together at the weld location. The electrodes apply mechanical pressure (squeeze force) to the sheets.
2. **Squeeze:** The electrodes squeeze the sheets together, ensuring good electrical contact and proper joint geometry.
3. **Weld:** A high current (5,000–100,000 amperes, depending on material and machine) passes through the electrodes and sheets for a very short time (milliseconds to seconds). The highest resistance in the circuit is at the metal-to-metal interface — heat concentrates there.
4. **Nugget formation:** The heat melts a small ellipsoidal "nugget" of metal at the interface. The nugget is the weld.
5. **Hold:** Current stops but pressure continues while the nugget solidifies under pressure. This produces a dense, sound weld nugget.
6. **Release:** Electrodes open and advance to the next weld location.

The entire cycle for a typical automotive spot weld takes 0.1–0.5 seconds.

### Why Copper Electrodes?

Copper has very high electrical conductivity (low resistance) and relatively good thermal conductivity. This means:
- Minimal heat is generated in the electrodes themselves
- Heat concentrates at the metal-metal interface, not in the electrode
- Electrodes stay cool enough to maintain consistent contact geometry

Copper electrodes still wear — the contact face deforms and oxidizes with repeated use. Electrode dressing (grinding or milling the contact face back to shape) is a routine maintenance task in production spot welding.

## Key Spot Welding Parameters

Three parameters control spot weld quality:

**Current (amperage):** Determines how much heat is generated. Too low = undersized nugget, no fusion. Too high = expulsion (metal squirting out from the joint), surface indentation, and electrode sticking.

**Time:** How long current flows. Longer time at the same current = more heat = larger nugget. Very short times are used in modern pulsed welding controllers.

**Force (pressure):** Electrode clamping force during welding. Low force increases contact resistance (generates more heat in the right place) but allows expulsion at high currents. High force stabilizes the weld but requires higher current.

Modern industrial spot welders use microprocessor controllers to manage precise combinations of current, time (including rise and fall times), and force for each weld type.

## Spot Weld Nugget Size and Quality

The weld nugget is the actual fusion zone between the sheets. Quality spot welds have:

- **Nugget diameter** equal to approximately 4–5 times the square root of the sheet thickness (√t × 4 for minimum, √t × 5 for recommended)
- **Full penetration through both sheets** with a fusion zone touching both sheet surfaces
- **No expulsion** — no metal splattered outside the joint
- **Minimal surface indentation** — the electrode face impression should be shallow

### Testing Spot Welds

Spot weld quality testing methods:
- **Peel test** — One sheet is peeled back from the other. A good weld shows a nugget torn from one sheet (nugget pull-out). A bad weld shows interface separation (no fusion).
- **Chisel test** — A chisel is hammered between the sheets near the weld. Good welds show nugget pull-out when sheets separate.
- **Destructive tensile test** — Measures actual joint strength
- **Ultrasonic testing** — Non-destructive evaluation of nugget size and integrity. Standard in quality-critical automotive production.

## Applications of Resistance Spot Welding

### Automotive Manufacturing

The automotive industry is the largest user of resistance spot welding. A typical passenger car body-in-white (BIW) contains **3,000–7,000 spot welds**, applied by robotic welding systems. Automotive spot welding involves:
- Galvanized steel (most exterior panels)
- Dual-phase and advanced high-strength steels (structural members)
- Aluminum (increasing use in body panels for lightweight designs)

Automotive robots apply weld schedules calibrated to each specific stack-up (combination of sheet gauges and coatings) to produce consistent, certified welds.

### Sheet Metal Fabrication

Cabinet manufacturing, appliance fabrication, HVAC equipment, office furniture, and computer enclosures use spot welding extensively. These applications involve:
- Thinner gauges (18–24 gauge mild steel or galvanized)
- Lower required strengths than automotive structural
- Often manual (not robotic) machines in smaller shops

### Aerospace Sheet Metal

Aircraft sheet metal joining uses resistance spot welding for:
- Skin-to-stringer connections (stringers are internal stiffeners)
- Overlap joints in fuselage skin panels
- Light structural attachments

Aerospace spot welding is held to much stricter quality standards than automotive — each weld may require radiographic inspection.

### Battery Tab Welding

Lithium battery packs for electric vehicles and consumer electronics use resistance spot (or related) welding to connect battery cells to busbars and tabs. This is a specialized, precision application with extremely tight heat input requirements to avoid damaging cells.

## DIY Spot Welders for Home Shops

Commercial spot welders are large, expensive pieces of equipment (pneumatic-operated arm welders starting at $3,000+). But for home shop auto body work, more accessible options exist.

### Body Shop Spot Welders

**Tip-style spot welders** are the standard for auto body repair. One electrode is in the gun body; the second is on a backing arm that accesses the back side of the panel.

**Recommended:** [Eastwood Spot Weld Stitch Welder](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B001XXQNC2/?tag=theweldersguide-20) — designed for automotive sheet metal repair. Handles most factory spot weld replacement tasks.

**Stud welder / dent puller style:** A single-sided spot welder that welds a stud or tab to the surface and allows dent pulling. Also used for welding through holes when back side access is impossible.

### Capacitor Discharge (CD) Spot Welders

Capacitor discharge welders store energy in capacitors and release it in a very short, high-power burst. They produce:
- Very short weld time (milliseconds)
- Minimum heat input and distortion
- Suitable for joining thin to thick, copper-to-copper, and difficult material combinations

The [Sunstone Pulse Arc Welder](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B07GPBHJ4G/?tag=theweldersguide-20) is a CD-type machine used for battery tab welding and precision sheet metal joining.

### DIY Spot Welder from Microwave Oven Transformer (MOT)

A DIY spot welder built from microwave oven transformers is a popular home shop project. A rewound MOT produces the high current needed for spot welding at low voltage (2–3V), drawing from standard household current.

**Capability:** Adequate for 18–22 gauge mild steel. Not suitable for galvanized (coating disrupts contact), stainless, or thick material.

**Resources:** Numerous guides and YouTube videos document MOT welder builds. Required parts: 1–2 MOTs, heavy copper cable (4/0 AWG minimum), and electrode material (copper rod or RWMA Class 2 electrode copper).

**Safety consideration:** MOT welding machines operate at lethal voltages (2,000V+) on the primary side. The transformer must be rewound and handled with care. Do not work on MOT welders unless you understand electrical safety.

## Resistance Spot Welding vs. MIG Plug Welding

For automotive repair, **MIG plug welding** (MIG welding through a drilled hole) is the common alternative to spot welding when a factory spot welder is not available. MIG plug welding:
- Does not require special equipment beyond a standard MIG welder
- Produces welds that are structurally comparable to spot welds in the same location
- Is acceptable per OEM repair standards (I-CAR and manufacturer repair manuals specify acceptable weld methods)
- Requires drilling appropriately sized holes (5/16"–3/8" typical) in the outer panel

Spot welding is faster and produces less visible evidence of repair; MIG plug welding is more accessible and produces equivalent structural results. Both are used in production automotive repair.

## Resistance Seam Welding

A related process, resistance seam welding, uses wheel-shaped electrodes that roll along a joint and produce a series of overlapping spot welds — effectively a continuous weld seam. Applications:
- Gas tanks and fuel containers (hermetically sealed)
- Radiators and heat exchangers
- Steel drums and containers
- HVAC ductwork requiring leakproof seams

Seam welding equipment is specialized and not common in general fabrication shops.

## Summary: When to Use Spot Welding

| Application | Spot Welding Suitability |
|---|---|
| Automotive panel replacement | Excellent — factory repair method |
| Sheet metal box fabrication | Excellent — fast, flat joints |
| Structural steel | Not suitable — not enough strength or size |
| Aluminum sheet | Possible but requires different parameters (Al RSW) |
| Stainless steel | Yes, with appropriate current adjustment |
| Thick plate (3/16"+) | Not practical — need fusion welding processes |
| Battery tabs and electronics | Excellent (CD welding) |
| Pipe and tube joints | Not suitable — geometry doesn't allow electrode access |

Resistance spot welding excels in sheet metal lap joint applications and is the most efficient method for joining thin gauge steel in production environments. For general fabrication and structural work, fusion welding processes are appropriate.
