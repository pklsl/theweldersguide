# Top Welding Certifications Beyond AWS D1.1

AWS D1.1 (Structural Welding Code — Steel) is the first certification most welders pursue, and for good reason — it is the most widely recognized welding performance qualification in construction and general fabrication. But the welding industry uses many codes, each governing a specific application, material, or industry. Knowing which certifications matter for your target work opens significant career and earning opportunities.

This guide covers the most important welding certifications and credentials beyond AWS D1.1.

## Welding Codes vs. Welder Certifications

Before diving into specific credentials, it is important to understand the distinction:

**Welding codes** are the written standards that define how welding must be performed — joint design, preheat requirements, inspection criteria, acceptable defects. Examples: AWS D1.1, ASME Section IX, API 1104.

**Welder performance qualifications** are tests conducted to verify that a specific welder can produce welds meeting the requirements of a specific code, using a specific process, on a specific material, in a specific position. These "certifications" expire if you stop performing that type of work or fail to maintain continuity records.

**Welding Inspector certifications** (like CWI) certify that the individual has the knowledge to inspect and accept or reject welds to code requirements. These are earned by examination, not performance testing.

## ASME Section IX — Pressure Vessel and Piping Welding

**Governing body:** American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME)
**Applicable to:** Boilers, pressure vessels, pressure piping, heat exchangers

ASME Section IX is the welding qualification standard for pressure equipment governed by the ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code (BPVC). If the equipment holds pressure — steam, hot water, process gases, liquids under pressure — ASME Section IX likely governs the welding.

### What ASME IX Covers

- Welding performance qualifications (WPQ) by process, material group, and position
- Procedure qualification requirements (PQR) and welding procedure specifications (WPS)
- Groove weld and fillet weld qualification ranges
- Essential variables — changes to these variables require requalification

### How to Qualify

1. An employer must have a qualified Welding Procedure Specification (WPS) supported by a Procedure Qualification Record (PQR)
2. The welder performs a test coupon to the WPS
3. Coupons are tested by mechanical testing (bend test) and may be radiographically inspected
4. Passing welders receive a Welder Performance Qualification (WPQ)

ASME IX qualifications are not portable between employers without re-testing — each employer must establish their own qualification records.

### Industries Using ASME IX

- Power generation (steam plants, nuclear plants)
- Oil refining and petrochemical processing
- Chemical plants
- Pharmaceutical and food processing
- Industrial steam systems

## API 1104 — Pipeline Welding

**Governing body:** American Petroleum Institute (API)
**Applicable to:** Onshore and offshore oil and gas transmission pipelines

API Standard 1104 governs field welding of pipelines carrying petroleum products. It is separate from ASME IX and applies specifically to pipeline construction and maintenance.

### Key Characteristics of API 1104

- Focused on field (not shop) welding conditions
- Emphasizes the downhill (3G-down) welding technique standard in pipeline construction
- Requires radiographic (x-ray) testing of test joints
- Both root-pass-only and full-weld qualification options

### API 1104 Qualification Process

1. Weld a test coupon per the applicable WPS
2. Visual inspection of the completed weld
3. Radiographic examination
4. Mechanical testing (nick-break, bend tests)
5. Documentation in welder qualification record

API 1104 qualifications cover specific pipe diameter and wall thickness ranges.

### Where API 1104 Matters

Required for work on:
- Cross-country natural gas and crude oil pipelines
- Offshore pipeline construction
- Pipeline repair and maintenance contracts
- DOT Part 192 (natural gas) and Part 195 (hazardous liquids) regulated work

## AWS Certified Welding Inspector (CWI)

**Governing body:** American Welding Society (AWS)
**Type:** Knowledge-based certification examination

The CWI is one of the most valuable credentials in the welding industry because it does not require daily welding — it certifies your knowledge of welding inspection, codes, and quality assurance. CWIs are in consistent demand in fabrication shops, construction, pipeline, and third-party inspection.

### CWI Requirements

- **Education and experience combination:** The minimum requirement is a combination of welding education and work experience. Applicants with a welding engineering degree need only 1 year of experience; high school graduates with no welding-related education need 5 years of experience.
- **Examination:** Three-part exam:
  1. **Fundamental exam** — Welding processes, metallurgy, codes, and inspection methods. Open-book.
  2. **Practical exam** — Hands-on weld sample identification and measurement. Closed-book.
  3. **Code book exam** — Specific code-book application (typically AWS D1.1). Open-book.

### CWI Maintenance

CWI certification is valid for 3 years and must be renewed through continuing education and examination.

### CWI Pay

CWIs earn $30–$55/hour ($60,000–$110,000 annually) in employment. Independent CWI contractors providing inspection services to fabrication shops and construction sites earn $400–$800/day.

### Senior Certified Welding Inspector (SCWI)

The SCWI is an advanced credential for experienced CWIs with additional examination requirements and 6 years of post-CWI welding inspection experience.

## AWS Certified Welding Educator (CWE)

**Type:** Knowledge and teaching credential

The CWE recognizes welding educators who teach welding in vocational, community college, and university settings. Requirements include welding knowledge and experience in welding education.

For welders who transition into teaching — community colleges, vocational programs, union apprenticeship programs — the CWE validates their instructional qualifications.

## AWS Certified Welding Sales Representative (CWSR)

**Type:** Industry knowledge credential

The CWSR is designed for welding equipment and consumables sales professionals who need to demonstrate technical competency. While not directly applicable to welders in the shop, experienced welders who move into distributor or manufacturer representative roles often pursue this.

## AWS Certified Welding Supervisor (CWS)

**Type:** Supervisory credential

The CWS certifies welders in supervisory roles — lead welders, foremen, and welding supervisors. Requires passing the same fundamental examination as the CWI plus practical welding knowledge.

Applicable to welders moving into shop supervision, project management, and quality assurance roles.

## AWS D1.2 — Structural Welding of Aluminum

AWS D1.1 covers steel; **AWS D1.2** covers aluminum. As aluminum use expands in transportation, marine, and construction applications, D1.2 qualification is increasingly valuable.

Key differences from D1.1:
- AC TIG welding is standard (aluminum requires AC for oxide cleaning)
- Different preheat requirements
- Different acceptance criteria for porosity (aluminum is more porous than steel)
- Different pre-qualified joint designs

D1.2 qualification is valuable for welders in marine fabrication, trailer manufacturing, and specialty vehicle industries.

## AWS D9.1 — Sheet Metal Welding

**D9.1** governs welding of sheet metal (material under 10 gauge). Applicable to HVAC ductwork fabrication, light gauge framing, and architectural sheet metal.

## ASME Section VIII — Pressure Vessel Fabrication

While ASME IX governs welding qualification, **ASME Section VIII** governs the design and fabrication of pressure vessels. Shops that fabricate code-stamped pressure vessels must operate under an ASME Certificate of Authorization (the "U Stamp" for unfired pressure vessels).

For welders working in ASME-stamped shops, understanding the relationship between Section VIII and Section IX is important career knowledge.

## Military Welding Certifications

### MIL-STD-1 Welding

Military welding standards are published by the Department of Defense. Key standards:
- **MIL-STD-1418** — Welding, flash, and induction welding processes
- **DOD-STD-2219** — Welding, high quality, aerospace and defense

### NAVSEA Standard Items

Naval shipbuilding welding is governed by NAVSEA (Naval Sea Systems Command) standard items that reference ASME IX, MIL-STD specifications, and Navy-specific requirements. Welders on Navy ship construction must qualify to NAVSEA requirements.

### Secret or Top Secret Clearance

Classified defense work (submarines, certain naval vessels, aerospace platforms) requires security clearance in addition to welding qualifications. Cleared welders earn a significant premium.

## AWS D17.1 — Aerospace Fusion Welding

**AWS D17.1** governs fusion welding for aerospace applications. It is the standard for commercial aerospace welding where MIL-spec does not apply. Key characteristics:
- Extensive pre-weld cleaning requirements
- Strict shielding gas purity requirements
- Post-weld heat treatment requirements for many alloys
- Rigorous non-destructive testing (NDT) requirements

D17.1 qualifications are required at aerospace fabrication shops, repair stations (FAA Part 145 repair stations), and aerospace tier suppliers.

## Choosing Your Certification Path

| Target Industry | Key Certifications |
|---|---|
| Construction | AWS D1.1, D1.4 (rebar) |
| Pressure equipment | ASME Section IX |
| Pipeline | API 1104 |
| Aerospace | AWS D17.1, company WPS qualification |
| Marine/shipbuilding | NAVSEA standards, AWS D1.2 (aluminum) |
| Nuclear | ASME Section III, company WPS |
| Inspection career | AWS CWI |
| Aluminum structures | AWS D1.2 |
| Military vehicles | MIL-STD specifications |

## How to Pursue Welding Certifications

1. **Identify your target industry** and research the governing code.
2. **Find training resources:**
   - AWS district representatives can direct you to testing facilities and education resources.
   - Community colleges often offer certification prep courses.
   - Union apprenticeship programs include relevant certification prep.
3. **Practice to the code** — do not just practice welding; practice making welds that comply with the specific code's acceptance criteria.
4. **Find a qualified testing facility** — most certifications require testing at an approved laboratory or employer testing station.
5. **Maintain your certifications** — track expiration dates and maintain continuity records (proof you are actively performing certified work).

Certifications are investments in your career. Each credential you earn expands the range of work you can legally perform and justifies higher rates. The CWI alone is arguably the most valuable credential in the industry for long-term career and earning flexibility.
