# Aerospace Welding: Standards, Materials, and Career Paths

Aerospace welding sits at the top of the welding profession in terms of technical demand, required precision, and the consequences of failure. A weld on a commercial aircraft engine component, a spacecraft pressure vessel, or a defense missile housing is held to standards that most industrial welding cannot approach. The payoff is a career that commands premium wages, offers genuine intellectual challenge, and operates in some of the most sophisticated manufacturing environments in the world.

This guide covers what aerospace welding involves, the standards that govern it, the materials you will weld, and how to break into the field.

## What Makes Aerospace Welding Different

Three characteristics set aerospace welding apart from other industries:

**1. Zero tolerance for certain defects.** In structural and pressure-retaining aerospace welds, defects that would be acceptable under AWS D1.1 or ASME IX are rejectable. A 1/16-inch porosity pit in a structural steel weld is inconsequential; in an aircraft engine component, it is cause for scrapping a multi-thousand-dollar part.

**2. Exotic and difficult materials.** Aerospace applications demand materials with exceptional strength-to-weight ratio, temperature resistance, or corrosion resistance. Titanium, Inconel, 300-series stainless, 2219 and 7075 aluminum alloys, and refractory metals are routine in aerospace fabrication.

**3. Extensive documentation and traceability.** Every aerospace weld is traceable — the welder, machine settings, filler metal heat number, shielding gas lot, inspection results, and acceptance documentation create a complete record for each weld. This documentation traceability is mandated by aerospace quality management systems (AS9100) and government regulations (FAA, DoD).

## Governing Standards

### AWS D17.1 — Fusion Welding for Aerospace Applications

**AWS D17.1** is the primary welding standard for commercial aerospace fusion welding. Published by the American Welding Society, it covers:
- Welder and welding operator qualification requirements
- Process control requirements
- Inspection and acceptance criteria (significantly stricter than structural codes)
- Heat treatment requirements
- Documentation and records

AWS D17.1 is referenced by Boeing, Airbus tier suppliers, and general aviation manufacturers. FAA Part 145 repair stations (MRO facilities) frequently operate under D17.1 for airframe and engine welded component repair.

### MIL-STD-1942 and MIL-W-8604

Military welding standards apply to DoD programs:
- **MIL-STD-1942** — General welding requirements for military equipment
- **MIL-W-8604** — Welding of aluminum alloys for aircraft

Defense programs (fighters, missiles, rotorcraft) typically reference applicable MIL-SPECs or draw-specific requirements in lieu of D17.1.

### NADCAP (National Aerospace and Defense Contractors Accreditation Program)

NADCAP is the industry-managed accreditation program for special processes in aerospace manufacturing. NADCAP accreditation for welding means a shop has been audited against defined requirements for process control, equipment calibration, welder qualification, and inspection.

Boeing, Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, and other primes require their suppliers to hold NADCAP accreditation for welding. For a welding shop entering the aerospace supply chain, obtaining NADCAP accreditation for welding is a significant business milestone.

The NADCAP audit covers:
- Welder qualification records (current and documented per D17.1 or applicable spec)
- Welding equipment calibration records
- Shielding gas purity documentation
- Filler metal certification and traceability
- Inspection equipment calibration
- Process control documentation

### AS9100

AS9100 is the quality management system standard for the aerospace industry (based on ISO 9001 with aerospace-specific additions). Aerospace customers increasingly require their supply chain to hold AS9100 certification. AS9100 addresses how the entire quality management system operates — not just welding, but purchasing, design control, non-conformance management, and continuous improvement.

## Materials in Aerospace Welding

### Titanium

Titanium is the preeminent aerospace material — used in airframe structure, engine components, fasteners, and spacecraft pressure vessels. Its exceptional strength-to-weight ratio and temperature resistance are unmatched by any common metal.

**Common aerospace titanium alloys:**
- **Ti-6Al-4V (Grade 5)** — The most common aerospace titanium. Used in engine fan blades, aircraft structure, landing gear components.
- **Ti-6Al-4V ELI (Grade 23)** — Extra-low interstitials for improved fracture toughness. Used in critical structural and medical applications.
- **Ti-3Al-2.5V (Grade 9)** — Hydraulic tubing and smaller-diameter structural tube.
- **Commercially pure Ti (Grade 2)** — Corrosion-resistant applications, chemical processing.

Titanium welding in aerospace requires:
- 100% argon shielding (trailing shield + back purge)
- Obsessive cleanliness (dedicated tools, gloves, acetone cleaning)
- Color-coded weld acceptance (silver to light straw only)
- Low heat input and controlled interpass temperatures for Grade 5

### Nickel Superalloys (Inconel, Waspaloy, Rene)

Jet engine hot-section components operate at temperatures that exceed the capability of steel or titanium. Nickel superalloys maintain strength at temperatures up to 80–90% of their melting point:
- **Inconel 718** — Engine casings, turbine disks, fasteners
- **Inconel 625** — Combustion liners, exhaust components, pressure-retaining parts
- **Waspaloy** — Turbine disks and blades (age-hardenable superalloy)
- **Rene 41, Rene 80, Mar-M-247** — High-temperature turbine section components

Superalloy welding requires:
- TIG welding with precise heat input control
- Matching or slightly over-alloyed filler metals
- Interpass temperatures typically below 200–300°F
- Post-weld heat treatment for many alloys
- Extensive inspection (fluorescent penetrant, x-ray)

### Aluminum Alloys

Aluminum use in aerospace is extensive for structures where strength-to-weight ratio matters and temperatures are not extreme (generally below 250°F sustained):
- **2024-T3** — Fuselage skin, wing structures (high strength)
- **7075-T6** — Highly stressed structural components (highest strength aluminum alloy commonly used)
- **6061-T6** — Lower-stress structures, access panels, fittings
- **2219** — Cryogenic fuel tanks (Saturn V, Space Shuttle, current launch vehicles) — excellent cryogenic toughness

**2024 and 7075 are not weldable by conventional fusion welding** (they are highly crack-sensitive). Structural joints in these alloys are fastened, not welded. Welding in aerospace aluminum structures is typically limited to 6061 and 2219 (for fuel tanks).

### Stainless Steel

300-series stainless steel is used for:
- Fuel and hydraulic lines (316L, 321)
- Exhaust and pressure-retaining components
- Fairings and non-structural applications

### Refractory Metals

For the most extreme temperature applications (rocket nozzles, reentry thermal protection, electron beam optics):
- **Tungsten (W)** — Highest melting point metal. Electron beam welded.
- **Molybdenum (Mo)** — Rocket nozzle inserts, high-temperature vacuum components.
- **Niobium (Columbium)** — Rocket nozzle extensions, cryogenic superconductors.

Refractory metal welding requires specialized processes (electron beam, plasma arc, or laser) in protective atmosphere or vacuum environments.

## Qualification and Certification for Aerospace Welding

### Welder Qualification per AWS D17.1

D17.1 welder qualification is more extensive than D1.1:
- Position qualifications are the same concept, but radiographic examination of the test coupon is typically required
- Specific qualification is required by material group AND process
- Qualification records must be maintained with documented welding on qualified processes — lapse of 6+ months in a process typically requires requalification

### Company-Specific Qualifications

Major aerospace OEMs (Boeing, Lockheed, Raytheon) and their auditing authorities (NADCAP, Primes) may require additional company-specific or drawing-specific qualifications beyond D17.1. These are the "gold standard" qualifications that matter most to aerospace employers.

### FAA Airmen Certificate (Welder A&P)

Welders working on FAA-certificated aircraft and components through FAA Part 145 repair stations must meet qualifications specified in the station's approved repair station manual (RSM). The RSM references D17.1 or other applicable standards and documents qualification requirements specific to the certificate holder.

## Career Path into Aerospace Welding

### Starting Position: General Welding

Most aerospace welders begin in general welding (structural, manufacturing, or job shop) and develop the fundamental skills — TIG proficiency, precision, documentation discipline — before moving to aerospace.

Starting in general welding and developing TIG proficiency on stainless and aluminum over 2–4 years gives you the foundation for aerospace entry.

### Entry into Aerospace Manufacturing

Tier 2 and Tier 3 aerospace suppliers (companies that make components for companies that supply Boeing, Lockheed, etc.) are the most common entry point. These shops do precision TIG welding on aircraft components and assemblies.

To be competitive for entry-level aerospace welding positions:
- Solid TIG welding skills across multiple materials
- AWS D1.1 3G/4G performance qualification (for baseline credentialing)
- Clean background (defense work requires DoD security clearance processing)
- Attention to documentation and quality discipline

### Advancement Path

1. **Entry aerospace welder** — TIG welding on non-critical components, developing process knowledge
2. **Qualified welder** — D17.1 qualifications across multiple materials
3. **Senior welder** — Complex assemblies, difficult materials, mentoring junior welders
4. **Lead/setup role** — Develops weld procedures, trains welders, interfaces with quality
5. **Quality inspector / CWI** — Inspection and quality assurance responsibility

### Salary Range

**Entry aerospace welder:** $20–$30/hour
**Qualified TIG welder (2–5 years experience):** $28–$45/hour
**Senior aerospace welder:** $40–$65/hour
**Lead/specialist:** $50–$80/hour

Geographic concentration: Aerospace welding jobs concentrate where aerospace manufacturing is centered — Seattle (Boeing/Spirit), Wichita (Cessna/Beechcraft/Spirit), Fort Worth/Dallas (Lockheed, Bell), Los Angeles/Palmdale (Northrop, Boeing), Connecticut (Pratt & Whitney), and Ohio (GE Aviation).

## Final Thoughts

Aerospace welding demands more from welders than any other field — more precision, more documentation discipline, more knowledge of materials, and more rigorous qualification. In return, it offers some of the highest wages in the trade, work on some of the most sophisticated technology manufactured today, and the knowledge that your welds are going somewhere remarkable — into orbit, across oceans, or into combat at Mach 2.

The path into aerospace welding is patient, progressive, and worth it for welders who bring the dedication it requires.
