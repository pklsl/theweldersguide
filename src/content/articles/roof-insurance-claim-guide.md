A major storm rolls through, and now you're looking at missing shingles, damaged gutters, or worse. Filing an insurance claim for roof damage can feel overwhelming — especially when you're also dealing with contractors showing up at your door and an insurance company that may be motivated to minimize your payout.

Here's how to navigate the process effectively and protect your interests.

## Step 1: Assess and Document the Damage First

Before you call your insurance company, document everything. This protects you if there's any dispute later about the extent of damage.

**From the ground**, photograph and video:
- Missing or visibly damaged shingles
- Bent or crushed gutters
- Damaged roof vents, skylights, or flashing
- Any interior water stains visible through windows
- Hail marks on soft metal surfaces (AC units, mailbox, downspouts) — these serve as proxy evidence for the storm's severity

**Inside the attic:**
- Water stains, wet insulation, or daylight visible through the deck
- Photograph the date/time on a phone screen alongside damage photos for timestamped evidence

**Date and weather stamps matter.** Your insurer will request the storm date. Match your documentation to the weather event.

## Step 2: Review Your Policy Before Calling

Understanding your policy before you speak to your insurer helps you know what to expect.

Key terms to look for:

- **ACV vs. RCV:** "Actual Cash Value" pays the depreciated value of your roof (a 15-year-old roof gets significantly less than replacement cost). "Replacement Cost Value" covers the full cost of replacement minus your deductible. RCV policies are significantly more valuable.
- **Deductible:** Most policies have a standard deductible, but many policies also have a **separate wind/hail deductible** that may be 1–5% of your home's insured value — far higher than the standard deductible. Check yours.
- **Coverage exclusions:** Age-related deterioration, poor maintenance, and gradual damage are typically excluded. Your claim must show the damage resulted from a specific storm event, not general wear.
- **Filing deadline:** Many policies require claims to be filed within 12 months of the storm event, but some state laws extend this. Don't delay.

## Step 3: Contact Your Insurer to File the Claim

Call your insurance company's claims line or file online. You'll need:
- Your policy number
- The date of the storm
- A brief description of the damage
- Your documentation (photos and video)

Your insurer will assign a claim number and schedule an adjuster visit.

## Step 4: Get Your Own Roofing Estimate

Don't wait for the adjuster to be your only source of information about what's damaged or what repairs cost.

**Get 2–3 written estimates** from local, licensed roofing contractors before or concurrent with the adjuster visit. These give you:
- An independent assessment of the damage
- A realistic sense of what proper repairs cost
- Documentation to dispute the insurer's estimate if it's too low

The adjuster works for the insurance company. They are trained to assess damage accurately, but their estimate can miss items or undervalue work — especially in a high-volume post-storm situation.

## Step 5: The Adjuster Visit

The insurance adjuster will inspect your roof. You (or your contractor) should be present during this inspection.

**What to do:**
- Walk the roof with the adjuster if you're comfortable, or have your contractor do so.
- Point out all documented damage and don't assume the adjuster will find everything.
- Ask specifically about soft metal hits (gutters, drip edge, vent caps) — these are often overlooked but are relevant to storm damage documentation.
- Ask when you'll receive the written estimate and claim decision.

**What not to do:**
- Don't sign anything with the adjuster at this stage.
- Don't let a contractor be in charge of your claim — you are the policyholder.
- Don't accept a verbal estimate as final.

## Step 6: Review the Adjuster's Estimate

You'll receive a written "scope of loss" from your insurer detailing what damage they're covering and what they'll pay. Review it against your contractor estimates.

Common discrepancies to look for:
- **Missing line items:** Torn or lifted ridge cap, damaged flashing, gutters, or vents not included
- **Depreciation applied:** On an RCV policy, depreciation is withheld initially and released after repairs are completed — this is normal
- **Overhead and profit (O&P):** Large jobs often require general contractor coordination; O&P should be included for complex repairs
- **Unit costs:** Prices per square (100 sq ft) should reflect local labor and material costs, not national averages

If the estimate seems low, request a **re-inspection** or provide your contractor's estimate as a counter.

## Step 7: Dispute If Necessary

If your insurer's estimate is significantly below actual repair costs:

1. **Contact your claims adjuster in writing** (email is fine) with a specific list of disputed items and supporting documentation.
2. **Request a re-inspection** if major damage was missed.
3. **Invoke the appraisal clause** if your policy has one — this is a formal dispute resolution process where each party hires an appraiser and an umpire decides contested items.
4. **Consult a public adjuster** — these professionals represent policyholders (not insurers) in complex or disputed claims, typically for 10–15% of the final settlement. Use one if the gap is large.
5. **Contact your state insurance department** if you believe the insurer is acting in bad faith.

## Step 8: Hire a Contractor and Complete the Repairs

Once your claim is approved:

- **Choose your own contractor.** Your insurer may suggest preferred vendors, but you are not required to use them.
- **Sign a contract** that matches the scope in your approved claim estimate.
- **Don't sign an Assignment of Benefits (AOB)** document that transfers your claim rights to the contractor. This limits your ability to dispute issues later.
- **Keep records of all payments** and communications.

For RCV policies: your insurer typically releases the initial ACV payment, then releases the withheld depreciation (called "recoverable depreciation") after you submit proof that repairs are complete (contractor invoice + photos). Don't forget this step — many homeowners leave significant money unclaimed.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Filing unnecessary claims.** Minor damage that barely exceeds your deductible may not be worth filing — repeated claims can lead to policy non-renewal or premium increases.

**Letting a contractor file your claim.** You are the policyholder. No contractor should be communicating with your insurer on your behalf without your explicit involvement.

**Signing a contract before claim approval.** You should know what the insurer will pay before committing to a contractor's price.

**Agreeing to absorb the deductible.** If a contractor offers to "waive your deductible," understand this is typically insurance fraud — the contractor inflates the invoice by your deductible amount and you're the policyholder on record. This can result in claim denial and legal exposure.

**Missing documentation deadlines.** Submit your completion receipts to receive depreciation holdback promptly. There are usually 6–12 month windows to claim this.

---

Filing a roof insurance claim takes time and attention to detail, but the financial stakes are significant. Stay organized, document thoroughly, get independent estimates, and don't rush into signing anything. Your insurer has claims professionals on their side — make sure you understand your rights as well.
